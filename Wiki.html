Overview & brief history

Cars (automobiles) are wheeled motor vehicles used for passenger transportation. Invented in the late 19th century, they rapidly shaped industry, urban design, and mobility. This guide summarizes modern knowledge about cars: major types, mechanical & electrical systems, buying and maintenance advice, environmental considerations, and a practical glossary.

Evolution

From steam and electric experiments in the 1800s to gasoline-powered mass production (Ford Model T, 1908), and today's shift toward electrification and autonomy.

Who uses cars?

Private individuals, fleets, ride-hailing, logistics and emergency services. Cars vary dramatically by region due to regulation, infrastructure, and cultural norms.

Why it matters

Cars influence economy, city design, energy demand, and emissions. Understanding them helps with safer, cost-effective, and greener choices.

Types of cars (by body & purpose)

Hatchback

Compact, rear door that opens upwards; efficient in urban settings.

Sedan

Traditional 3-box design with separate trunk; balanced comfort and efficiency.

SUV / Crossover

Higher ride height, more cargo space; ranges from compact crossovers to large off-road SUVs.

Coupe & Convertible

Sportier styling; two doors often, convertibles for open-top driving.

Minivan / MPV

Passenger-first, sliding doors, family-oriented interior space.

Pickup Truck

Open cargo bed, strong towing/utility capability.

Sports & Supercars

Performance-focused with high horsepower, advanced aerodynamics, premium materials.

Key components & systems

A modern car is an integration of mechanical, electrical, and software systems. Below are the most important ones to understand.

Chassis & body

Frame or unibody, crash structures, doors, glass, and exterior panels.

Suspension & steering

Controls ride comfort, handling; includes springs, dampers, anti-roll bars, tie rods.

Brakes

Disc or drum; modern cars include ABS (Anti-lock Braking System) and electronic brake force distribution.

Electrical & electronics

Battery, alternator, wiring, ECUs (engine control units), infotainment, sensors, and advanced driver assistance systems (ADAS).

Powertrain

Engine(s), transmission/gearbox, driveshafts, differential(s) — explained in the powertrain section.

Climate & HVAC

Heating, ventilation, and air conditioning systems for comfort and defogging.

Powertrains: ICE, Hybrid, BEV

Powertrain refers to how a car generates and applies motive power. The three main families are internal combustion engine (ICE), hybrid, and battery-electric vehicle (BEV).

Internal Combustion Engine (ICE)

Runs on gasoline, diesel or alternative liquid/gaseous fuels. Key elements: cylinders, pistons, crankshaft, intake/exhaust, fuel system, and ignition. Pros: mature tech and fueling infrastructure. Cons: emissions and mechanical complexity.

Hybrid (HEV / PHEV)

Combines ICE and electric motor(s). HEVs recharge battery via regenerative braking and ICE; PHEVs (plug-in hybrids) add external charging capability for longer electric-only range.

Battery Electric Vehicle (BEV)

Fully electric, powered by large battery packs and one or more electric motors. Pros: zero tailpipe emissions, high torque, simple drivetrain. Consider charging infrastructure, battery life, and lifecycle emissions.

Safety & ratings

Key safety features and how cars are evaluated.

Active safety

Systems that help avoid collisions: ABS, traction control, lane-keep assist, automatic emergency braking (AEB).

Passive safety

Designed to protect occupants during a crash: seatbelts, airbags, crumple zones.

Crash testing

Independent organisations (e.g., Euro NCAP, IIHS, NHTSA) publish ratings from frontal, side, and rollover tests; check their latest results for specific models.

Maintenance & common troubleshooting

Regular maintenance keeps a car reliable and safe. Below are routine items and simple diagnostics.

Routine schedule

  • Oil & filter: every 5,000–15,000 km depending on oil type and manufacturer.
  • Tires: rotate every 8,000–12,000 km, check pressure monthly.
  • Brake fluid: typically every 2 years.
  • Air filter & cabin filter: inspect every 12 months.
  • Battery: test annually after 3 years of use.

Common problems & quick checks

Engine won't start — check battery and terminals. Overheating — check coolant level and radiator fan. Vibration — check tires and wheel balance. Warning lights — consult manual or diagnostic tool (OBD-II).

Diagnostic tools

OBD-II scanners read engine codes; many smartphone adapters & apps exist for basic diagnostics.

Buying guide (new vs used)

Deciding factors: budget, usage, fuel type, maintenance expectations, reliability, insurance, and resale value.

New car pros/cons

Pros: warranty, latest safety tech, no wear. Cons: depreciation (biggest hit in first 2–4 years).

Used car pros/cons

Pros: lower price and slower depreciation. Cons: potential hidden issues; get inspection and vehicle history (accidents, flood, title).

Checklist for test drive

  • Inspect body and tires
  • Listen for unusual noises
  • Test brakes at speed
  • Try infotainment and climate controls
  • Request vehicle history & service records

Fuels, energy & charging

Different fuels and energy sources have different costs, emissions, and infrastructure requirements.

Gasoline & diesel

Common, well-understood fuels with dense energy per volume. Diesel often offers greater fuel efficiency but can emit more NOx and particulates without modern after-treatment systems.

Compressed natural gas (CNG)

Lower CO₂ per kWh than gasoline in some cases; requires dedicated tanks and fueling stations.

Biofuels & synthetic fuels

Can reduce lifecycle emissions if produced sustainably; availability varies by region.

Electric (BEV)

Charged from the grid. Charging levels: AC Level 1 (slow), AC Level 2 (home/fast), DC fast charging (high power). Consider range, charge time, and battery warranty.

Environmental impact

Assess emissions over the vehicle lifecycle: manufacturing (especially battery production), use-phase fuel/energy, and end-of-life recycling. Electrification reduces tailpipe emissions but lifecycle benefits depend on electricity mix and recycling practices.

How to reduce impact

  • Choose efficient vehicles and maintain them
  • Use public transport, carpool, or active mobility when possible
  • For EVs, charge using low-carbon electricity where possible
  • Recycle and follow proper battery disposal/reuse programs

FAQ

How often should I change engine oil?
Follow the manufacturer's recommendation, commonly between 5,000–15,000 km depending on oil type and driving conditions.
Are electric cars cheaper to maintain?
Generally yes: fewer moving parts, no oil changes, and less brake wear due to regenerative braking. Battery health and replacement costs are the main long-term considerations.
How do I read tire size?
Example: 205/55R16 — 205mm width, 55% aspect ratio (height to width), radial construction, 16-inch wheel diameter.
